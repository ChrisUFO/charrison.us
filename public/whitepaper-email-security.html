Can Language Models Secure Your Inbox?

A Large-Scale Benchmark of LLM Email Threat Detection — ClUFO · March 28, 2026

Download Full Whitepaper (PDF)

Complete methodology, results, and analysis · ~4,800 words

Executive Summary

As organizations deploy LLM-powered agents to process emails — reading, summarizing, triaging, and acting on messages — email becomes a prompt injection attack surface. We benchmarked 97 LLM variants across 29 email security test cases evaluating their ability to distinguish benign correspondence from phishing, social engineering, reply-chain hijacking, OAuth consent phishing, and hidden instruction attacks.

Our results reveal a systematic sycophancy problem: models correctly identify threat indicators but assign high trust scores regardless. A Chain-of-Verification prompt combined with post-processing mitigates but does not eliminate this behavior.

The Sycophancy Problem

Models don't fail because they can't detect threats. They can. They'll write out a detailed analysis identifying impersonation, urgency language, and suspicious links — and then score trust 85.

They see the problem. They name the problem. And then they decide to trust it anyway.

Among top-performing models, 45–59% of evaluations needed post-processing to enforce the logical consequences of the concerns the model itself identified. Claude Opus 4.6 needed corrections on 48% of tests. GPT-5.4 on 45%.

This is not a capability problem. It is an alignment failure. The model has the information needed for correct classification. Sycophancy overrides its own analytical output.

Thinking Mode Is Counterproductive

44 models tested in both thinking and no-thinking modes:

  • No-think wins: 31 pairs (70.5%)
  • Think wins: 12 pairs (27.3%)
  • Tied: 1 pair (2.3%)

No-thinking variants also cost 20–46% less and complete 30–50% faster. Reasoning tokens make models more cooperative — which in a security context means more gullible.

Cost ≠ Security Quality

The most expensive model (Claude Opus 4.6, $25/M output) scores lower than the cheapest perfect model (Qwen3.5 Flash, $0.26/M output) — at 1/96th the cost. The cheapest zero-miss model overall (MiMo V2 Flash, $0.01/eval) costs 1/99th of Claude Opus per evaluation while scoring within 1 point.

The Hardest Threats

  • Reply-chain hijacking — 114 model-variants missed it. Nearly universal failure. Exploits legitimate conversation history.
  • Sophisticated phishing (realistic corporate language) — 114 misses. No obvious red flags. The email that would actually arrive in your inbox.
  • Hidden instructions in email footers — almost every model scored these at trust 85–97. Only GLM-5.1 caught it natively (trust 2/2/3, zero variance).

Recommendations

  1. Never rely on LLM judgment alone. Post-processing is non-negotiable.
  2. Use no-thinking mode for security tasks — cheaper, faster, more accurate.
  3. Budget models are sufficient. Pair them with robust post-processing.
  4. Reply-chain hijacking requires non-LLM defenses — thread integrity checks, sender authentication.
  5. Test your specific model. Results vary significantly between versions and providers.
